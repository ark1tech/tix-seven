System Services

TixSeven exposes a set of services organized around actors that interact with the system: attendees, event organizers, and the backend infrastructure. This section describes the services at the level of what the system provides and to whom. The mechanisms and internal workflows that produce these services are discussed in the succeeding System Architecture section.
Attendees
Identity-bound Ticket Issuance
An attendee buys a ticket by presenting their PhilSys QR code at the point of sale. The system validates their identity through the MOSIP Testbed, then issues a ticket bound to that verified identity for a specific event. No separate account, password, mobile application, or platform-specific credential is necessitated beyond the PhilSys ID the attendee already has. The issuance process therefore treats the national identity credential itself as the foundation of event access.
Identity-bound Ticket Persistence
Because the ticket is bound to the attendee’s identity rather than to a physical or digital artifact, it cannot be lost, stolen, or damaged independently of the ID itself. An attendee who misplaces a printed confirmation, misplaces a physical stub, or deletes an email receipt still retains full access to the event as long as they present the same PhilSys ID they used during purchase. The identity credential thus effectively becomes a persistent ticket reference.
Identity-based Gate Entry
At the venue, the attendee presents their PhilSys QR code to the gate scanner. The system revalidates the identity through MOSIP, checks for a valid unused ticket bound to that identity for the assigned event, and returns a grant or deny decision to the gate unit. Entry depends on successful identity verification and ticket validity at the moment of scan rather than on possession of a transferable ticket artifact.
Event Organizers
Authenticated Organizer Access
Organizers access TixSeven through an authenticated dashboard session. This access boundary keeps event, ticketing, gate, issuance, audit, and export capabilities behind organizer identity rather than exposing them as public attendee functions. Attendees do not need a TixSeven account or dashboard session; the organizer dashboard is the operational surface for managing the event environment around the attendee’s PhilSys credential.
Identity-Verified Ticket Issuance
Organizers can issue tickets with the assurance that each ticket is bound to a single verified identity and that no identity can hold more than one ticket per event. In the dashboard flow, this occurs when the organizer scans the attendee’s PhilSys QR code and submits it for verification through the system. This constraint is enforced during issuance through the uniqueness of the hashed identity-event credential, making it structurally impossible for a single attendee to obtain multiple tickets through the TixSeven issuance pipeline.
Configurable Event Operations
Organizers can prepare and maintain event records by defining the event’s venue, schedule, capacity, and descriptive details while the event remains configurable. They can also cancel an event when operations require it, which prevents further ticket issuance and gate acceptance according to the system’s lifecycle rules. This gives organizers a controlled way to shape the operating context before and during the event without bypassing backend enforcement.
Venue-Aligned Event Setup
Organizers select event venues from the system-maintained venue catalog rather than entering unconstrained location data. This keeps event setup aligned with the physical venues where gates are registered and assigned. The result is a consistent operational map between an event, its venue, and the gates that may legitimately serve it.
Gate Fleet Readiness
Organizers can register gates, assign them to the appropriate event, mark them online or offline, and remove them from use when they are no longer part of the operating environment. These actions prepare the physical gate fleet for admission while preserving system rules around venue consistency and active assignments. A gate must therefore be known, operational, and correctly assigned before it can participate in entry verification.
Deterministic Gate Verification
Every gate scan is evaluated in real time against the event’s active ticket registry. Hence, verification outcomes are produced by the system itself rather than by gate personnel, which removes discretionary judgment during entry operations. Organizers can observe these outcomes through the dashboard, but they do not override gate decisions there. A ticket either satisfies the verification conditions for that event or it does not, and the gate response follows directly from that result, which reduces inconsistent enforcement during high-volume entry periods and limits opportunities for preferential treatment or gate-level fraud.
Live Admission Oversight
Organizers can monitor issued, used, and unused tickets for an event alongside admission activity as scans are processed. This gives them situational awareness during high-volume entry without turning the dashboard into a manual decisioning surface. The service is observational in nature: it helps organizers understand event flow, capacity consumption, and verification activity while gate acceptance remains governed by the deterministic verification pipeline.
Audit-backed Dispute Resolution
Every scan attempt is permanently logged regardless of whether entry is granted, denied, interrupted by timeout, or terminated by an internal error. The resulting audit trail enables organizers to investigate disputes involving reused tickets, denied entry attempts, gate assignment issues, or hardware failures using recorded system activity instead of witness recollection alone. Organizers can review this activity in a searchable and filterable chronological record that supports live investigation and post-event reconciliation. The logs remain available even after identity-linked ticket data are removed during cleanup. Hence, operational review is facilitated without preserving long-term identity-to-attendance mappings.
Exportable Operational Records
Organizers can export ticket records, gate assignments, and verification logs for offline reporting, archival, or dispute investigation. This allows operational evidence to move into administrative workflows without giving organizers direct database access. Exported records preserve the same distinction as the live system: they support accountability and review without turning the dashboard into a substitute for backend verification or post-event privacy cleanup.
Privacy-Preserving Data Retention
After an event concludes and the cleanup lifecycle runs, the records connecting hashed identity tokens to ticket entries are deleted from the system. Organizers can therefore verify that the operational data required for entry has been removed once it is no longer needed. Ticket records and audit logs remain for administrative and compliance purposes, but neither retains sufficient information to reconstruct which national identity attended a particular event; this retention model allows operational accountability to persist without preserving long-term identity-to-attendance mappings.
Backend Services
These are the services the system provides to itself in order to support the above. They are not directly exposed to attendees or organizers but they form the operational foundation on which all other services rest.
MOSIP-mediated Identity Verification
At both issuance and entry, the system forwards the PhilSys QR payload to the MOSIP Testbed and receives a Partner-Specific User Token (PSUT) scoped specifically to TixSeven. This verification step is the root of every identity-dependent decision that the system makes. The system does not independently validate identity claims and treats MOSIP as the authoritative source for identity resolution. If MOSIP verification fails or becomes unavailable, identity-dependent actions cannot proceed.
Privacy-Preserving Identity Binding
The PSUT returned by MOSIP is never stored directly. Instead, the system combines the PSUT with the event identifier and derives a keyed hash that serves as the persistent identity-event link within the database. During gate verification, the same derivation process is repeated using the newly returned PSUT, and the resulting hash is matched against the stored record associated with the ticket. The integrity of this process hinges on the hash contract remaining identical between issuance and entry, including the algorithm, encoding, and system-level pepper that was utilized during derivation.
Persistent Identity Verification Logging
The backend maintains an append-only verification log that records all scan attempts, including requests that fail before gate or event resolution. Each entry captures the available operational context at the time of verification, including the raw gate identifier received from the hardware, resolved gate and event information where applicable, the ticket status at decision time, and the resulting verification outcome. Snapshot values are preserved even if the underlying records are later modified or deleted, which ensures that historical verification activity remains reviewable throughout the system’s operational lifetime.
Operational Lifecycle Enforcement
The backend manages the operational lifecycle of events, gates, and gate assignments. Events follow a controlled status progression from scheduled to active to concluded, with cancellation permitted before termination. Gates are restricted to a single active assignment at a time, and assignments are limited to events belonging to the same venue as the assigned gate. These operational rules are enforced through both service-layer validation and database constraints so that inconsistent or out-of-order state transitions cannot silently corrupt the system record.
